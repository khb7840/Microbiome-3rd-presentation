Abstract

  • The intestinal mucosal barrier is composed of epithelial cells that are protected by an overlying host-secreted mucous layer and functions as the first line of defence against pathogenic and non-pathogenic microorganisms.
  • Some microorganisms have evolved strategies to either survive in the mucosal barrier or circumvent it to establish infection.

Two mucous layers

  1. Firm inner layer: adherent to the epithelium and resistant to dense microbial colonization
  2. Loose outer layer: home to a dense population of commensal bacteria

Mucin monomer contains PTS domains;
which is a region of rich Proline(P), Threonine(T), and Serine(S).
The N-terminal and C-terminal domains mediate disulfide crosslinking to build a very large mucin network comprising thousands of monomers.

A series of glycosyl-transferase enzymes add thousands of individual O-linked glycans to the side chains of Ser and Thr residues that ultimately comprise up to 80% of the final MUC2 mass.

Synthesis of mucin glycans

  • GalNAc is first added by 1 of 20 different ppGALNACT enzymes to the hydroxyl group of Ser or Thr residues. This process builds a basal structure called Tn antigen.
  • Synthesis of core structures is initiated by a single glycosyl-transferase using Tn antigen as the substrate.
  • Core1: C1GALT1
  • Core3: C3GNT
  • These core structures can be further elongated into monoantennary or biantennary structures to make extended glycan chains.

Gut bacteria produce CAZymes that can catalyze glycans

Gut microbes utilizes CAZymes(Carbohydrate-active enzymes) for

  • Degradaing natural fibre polysaccharides from botanical sources
  • Degrading host mucosal glycans


Examples

  • Blood-group-removing GH98 enzymes liberate the terminal trisaccharide from either blood group A or B structures.
  • GH101 enzymes cleave peptide-attached GalNAc but only when it has an attached Tn antigen.
  • Zinc metalloprotease ZmpB from Clostridium perfringens cuts adjacent to glycosylated Ser and/or Thr residues.

Pathogens in the small intestine - M cell


M cell: Microfold cell


  • The major role of M cells is the sampling and transcytosis of antigens through the epithelial barrier.
  • M cells cover underlying lymphoid aggregates called Peyer's patches.

  • Certain pathogens use M cells to gain entry through the mucosal barrier.
  • Example: Salmonella enterica subsp. enterica serovar Typhimurium

Pathogens in the small intestine - M cell


Some pathogens invade through mucous layer using host cell surface proteins

  • Yersinia pseudotuberculosis uses its outer membrane protein invasin to transit through M cells into the underlying Peyer’s patch by interacting with β1 integrins expressed on the surface of the targeted cells
  • Listeria monocytogenes uses two of its main virulence factors, the surface proteins internalin A (InlA) and/or internalin B (InlB), to bind to E-cadherin and MET and invade epithelial cells, including M cells

Pathogens in the small intestine - Tight junction

Some pathogens secrete enzymes that cleave mucus or disrupt tight junctions in the underlying epithelial cells

Vibrio cholerae secretes a soluble Zn2+-dependent metalloproteinase (haemagglutinin protease (Hap)) that possesses mucinolytic activity


Zonula Occludens Toxin (ZOT) from Vibrio cholerae interacts with the extracellular domains of the tight junction proteins occludin and zonula occludens 1 protein (ZO1) to disrupt epithelial integrity.


Clostridium perfringens disrupts the tight junction using its enterotoxin targeting claudin.


A protozoan Giardia lamblia possesses abilities to degrade or disrupt mucus

Pathogens in the small intestine - Goblet cell


L. monocytogenes recognizes E-cadherin at the surface of villi located at the tip of mucus-expelling goblet cells

S. Typhimurium has evolved to circumvent host defences during invasion of the epithelial barrier.
This pathogen can evade the activity of Paneth cell-secreted cationic antimicrobial peptides (CAMPs), including defensins, by altering the anionic character of the cell surface.

Flagella grant an advantage to some pathogens during infection at mucosal surfaces

  • Some E. coli strains possess flagella termed H6 and H7, which are adhesive to mucins
  • Similar flagellum-related mucus adherence by Clostridium difficile, a nosocomial pathogen associated with antibiotic treatment, is thought to promote colonization by this organism, which can also form a biofilm near the epithelial surface.

Mucus disruption by pathogens


  • E. coli, Entamoeba histolytica and T. muris produce enzymes and lectins that either cleave or bind to mucins.
  • StcE, an E. coli metalloprotease, cleaves mucins, which helps the pathogen to reach epithelial cells.
  • T. muris secretes serine proteases that act on the amino-terminal disulfide crosslinking domain of MUC2

Tight junction disruption by pathogens


  • C. difficile and Clostridium perfringens secrete toxins that disrupt colonic tight junctions.
  • Outer membrane vesicles (OMVs) filled with proteases are secreted by Campylobacter jejuni and cleave the tight junction proteins E-cadherin and occludin.
  • C. difficile toxin A and toxin B disrupt the cellular actin cytoskeleton, which affects its interactions with tight junction components claudin and occludin

Human papillomavirus and the landscape of secondary genetic alterations in oral cancers

Genome research, 2019

2021-35389 Hyunbin Kim / 2021. 11. 16

Abstract

    SAMPLES
  • Genome comparison of 149 HPV-positive and 335 HPV-negative oral squamous cell carcinomas (OSCC) samples (tumor/normal pairs)
    • MUTATIONAL SIGNATURES
  • Different risk factors underlying the two cancer types were reflected in distinctive genomic mutational signatures
  • In HPV-positive OSCCs, the signatures of APOBEC cytosine deaminase editing, associated with anti-viral immunity, were strongly linked to overall mutational burden.
  • In contrast, in HPV-negative OSCCs, T>C substitutions in the sequence con- text 5′-ATN-3′ correlated with tobacco exposure.
    • GENES WITH MUTATIONS
  • Significant enrichment of somatic mutations was confirmed or newly identified in PIK3CA, KMT2D, FGFR3, FBXW7, DDX3X, PTEN, TRAF3, RB1, CYLD, RIPK4, ZNF750, EP300, CASZ1, TAF5, RBL1, IFNGR1, and NFKBIA.
  • High-ranking variant allele fractions implicated ZNF750, PIK3CA, and EP300 mutations as candidate driver events in HPV-positive cancers.
    • GENE EXPRESSION & CNV
  • Universal expression of HPV E6 and E7 oncogenes was a sine qua non of HPV-positive OSCCs
  • Frequent copy number changes were associated with concordant changes in gene expression. Chr 11q (including CCND1) and 14q (including DICER1 and AKT1) were recurrently lost in HPV-positive OSCCs, in contrast to their gains in HPV-negative OSCCs

Mechanism of HPV infection

Mechanism of HPV infection affecting the occurrence and development of cancer.

After HPV infects mucosal epithelial cells, its DNA is integrated into the host cell genome, thereby resulting in overexpression of E6 and E7 mRNA and altered function of various proteins in host cells.

E6 and E7 products degrades (or inactivates) pRb and P53 which act as tumor suppressor
