Review paper

Interactions of commensal and pathogenic microorganisms with the intestinal mucosal barrier

Nat Rev Microbiol, 2018

2021-35389 Hyunbin Kim / 2021. 11. 16

Abstract

  • The intestinal mucosal barrier is composed of epithelial cells that are protected by an overlying host-secreted mucous layer and functions as the first line of defence against pathogenic and non-pathogenic microorganisms.
  • Some microorganisms have evolved strategies to either survive in the mucosal barrier or circumvent it to establish infection.

The colonic epithelium consists of

  • enteroendocrine cells
    • produce and release hormones in response to a number of stimuli
  • tuft cells
    • chemosensory cells in the epithelial lining of the intestines
  • cup cells
    • have distinct cell types but functions are not well-known
  • goblet cells
    • secrete the mucus layer that protects the epithelium from the lumenal contents
  • sentinel goblet cells
    • guards the colonic crypt by triggering Nlrp6-dependent MUC2 secretion


These cells are located at the crypt entrance to reduce bacterial encroachment into the crypt.


MUC2 glycoproteins are a major constituent of colonic mucus

Mucin monomer contains PTS domains;
which is a region of rich Proline(P), Threonine(T), and Serine(S).
The N-terminal and C-terminal domains mediate disulfide crosslinking to build a very large mucin network comprising thousands of monomers.
